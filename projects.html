Improving Adherance to Medical Treatments - BMEG 357
Jan 2023 - Apr 2023
Skills: C, Python, Plotly, HTML, JavaScript, CSS, Engineering, Medical Engineering, Electrical Engineering, Microcontroller

This project was presented by Dr. Andrew Thamboo at St. Paul’s Sinus Centre in British Columbia during my studies, and focused on addressing patient noncompliance with post-operative treatment for chronic sinus disease.

The final design concept involved both hardware and software components. The hardware included a button attached to the sinus rinse bottle, soldered onto a microcontroller. This board communicated via Bluetooth with the users' phones, enabling data logging, encryption, and trend visualization on an app and web based dashboard.

The logged data was stored in a central database, allowing the app to provide reminders and positive reinforcement to encourage adherence. The data also enabled the creation of a dashboard for facilitating data visualization and analysis for doctors, allowing them to search and interpret patient data with ease, and ultimately develop personalized treatments.

This project was a cooperative team effort. I was incharge of the circuitry, communication between different components of the design, building the central database, populating the database with data for testing, and the doctors dashboard.

Solidworks was employed for 3D printing and prototyping the hardware casing. The hardware system included a microcontroller circuitry based on an Adafruit HUZZAH32 – ESP32 Feather Board. The code for controlling the microcontroller was written in C. On the software side, the patient user interface application was built using React.js, incorporating HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. The doctors dashboard was created using Plotly Dash. The data storage relied on google cloud services. The scripts to create data and populate the database were written in Python.

The Cancer Genome Atlas and bladder urothelial carcinoma - BMEG 310
Sep 2022 - Dec 2022
Skills: R, Data Science, Machine Learning, Bioinformatcs, Medical Engineering, Electrical Engineering

This project was presented in my introduction to bioinformatics course at university. Various bioinformatics methods were employed to identify biological trends and potential relationships between gene expressions, mutations, and patient demographics. The study utilized the Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) dataset for bladder cancer, examining 431 patients.

Through differential expression and pathway analyses, significant insights into gene expressions and mutations were obtained, leading to the identification of potential diagnostic markers and biological pathways relevant to the disease. The project's findings provide a strong foundation for understanding bladder cancer.

Differential expression (DE) analysis was performed using the DESeq2 package in R to identify genes with the highest variability between different patient classes. The data was prefiltered to reduce noise and improve computational efficiency. K-means clustering was then applied to classify the patients into four distinct clusters, and a subsequent heatmap analysis allowed for the identification of six gene clusters.

Pathway analysis was carried out on two patient clusters of interest using KEGG pathways and the R GAGE package. A total of 213 relevant pathways were found for these clusters, and the top 10 upregulated and downregulated pathways were further evaluated. Additionally, clinical data analysis and mutation data analysis were conducted to identify trends related to patient survival, tumor status, and mutation characteristics.

The project successfully established a relationship between the hsa0420 pathway and deletions in Chromosome 9, indicative of bladder urothelial carcinoma. The comprehensive analysis of gene expressions, mutations, and clinical data provided valuable insights into the disease patterns and potential diagnostic markers. The research project contributes to precision diagnostics for bladder cancer patients and lays the groundwork for further investigations into other biological applications and cancer types.

Anti-Covid Belt - CPSC 1491
Jan 2021 - Apr 2021
Skills: C, Medical Engineering, Electrical Engineering, Microcontroller

The Anti-Covid Belt is an engineering project developed in my time at Langara college with the purpose of providing an additional layer of protection against contracting the COVID-19 virus. The belt incorporates proximity sensors strategically placed around the user's waist to offer 360-degree coverage and trigger an alarm when someone breaches their personal space within a range of 6 feet or approximately 2 meters.

The main components of the Anti-Covid Belt include an Arduino Mega 2550 for processing data and controlling output, a breadboard for building the electrical circuit, a power bank for supplying power, ultrasonic sensors for measuring distances, a PIR sensor for detecting motion, a piezo buzzer for triggering alerts, and an HC-05 Bluetooth module for enabling dual-way communication with the user and controlling the sensors and buzzer.
